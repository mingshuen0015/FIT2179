Section 1: The National Overview & Financial Context

Cancer cases are rising rapidly. Although Malaysia's healthcare spending has been increasing year-by-year
matching our South-East Asian counterparts, age remains a major factor in cancer treatment.
Senior citizens face exponentially higher risks of developing cancer and experiencing mortality,
requiring us to be proactive in our approach to prevention and treatment.

Section 2: The Gender Divide

The burden weighs heavily on both genders, but men face higher risks of mortality across almost all states.
Tragically, late-stage detection is common amongst both genders, highlighting the urgent need for increased early screening initiatives.

Section 3: The Prominent Threats

Cancer is a complex disease with multiple contributing factors.
Understanding the most significant threats is crucial for developing effective prevention and treatment strategies.
Breast and colorectal cancers are the leading causes of mortality and also the most commonly diagnosed cancers in Malaysia,
with lung and liver cancers following closely behind.
Alarmingly, our historical trendlines show that there has been a sharp increase in the incidence of all the top 5 cancer types.
This shows that the threat of cancer is growing rapidly and it requires immediate attention.

Section 4: Mapping the Geographic Burden

Cancer incidence and mortality rates vary significantly across different regions of Malaysia.
Mapping these patterns helps identify areas with the highest burden and informs targeted intervention strategies.
Northern states like Perlis and Kedah suffer from the highest mortality rates across the state whilst certain districts in Eastern
Malaysia record the highest cancer mortality rates despite having an overall lower cancer mortality rate in the state.

Section 5: The Healthcare Response & Infrastructure

Are our hospitals properly equipped to handle the growing burden of cancer cases?
Surprisingly, higher per-capita healthcare spending on hospital beds do not guarantee a lower mortality rate from cancer.
In fact, some of the states with the highest per-capita spending on hospital beds also have some of the highest cancer mortality rates.
This suggests that there may be other factors involved in determining cancer mortality rates, such as the quality of care, access to early detection and treatment, and public health initiatives.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the rising burden of cancer in Malaysia requires a multifaceted approach that addresses not only the medical
aspects but also the social, economic, and environmental factors that contribute to its incidence and mortality rates.
Adding more hospital beds is simply not enough anymore. We must prioritize early detection and prevention strategies, invest in research and development for better treatments, and ensure equitable access to healthcare services across all regions of the country.
